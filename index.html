About me

I have always been fascinated by how powerful creative writing (as a tool to educate and entertain society) is. I have a keen interest in using writing to influence my reader's perspective, understanding and representation of my culture. Specifically, my interest lies in fusing oral historical tales with my creative re-imagination of Kenya’s history.

I studied creative writing and theatre as an undergraduate at Mount Holyoke College, and I'm currently an MFA graduate student at Florida Atlantic University, where I also teach English Composition. I have also worked as a playwright at the Arts Garage at Delray Beach, where I taught playwrighting to young artists and challenged them to fuse their imagination and collaborate to create their own production. While at Mount Holyoke College, I wrote and produced a play, ‘For Kenyan Girls’ - inspired by For Colored Girls, that tells the stories of oppressed women in Kenya. Currently, I am the choreographer for the play ‘For Colored Girls’ which will premiere at FAU in the Fall of 2019. My vision for Kenya and Africa at large is to see more people engage with the literary and theatrical world.

The Small Stories, and the big stories

This piece is a creative manifesto, an indictment and a criticism of one's professor's condescending, dismissive attitude toward what the piece calls 'The Small Stories' - those that do not square with white, colonial, hegemonic intepretations of history. At the same time, this writing is also a send up of the racism that persists in western literary culture. By examining the white, masculine maxim that calls 'to write big stories', we see a glimpse of the kinds of stories that risk going unvoiced and unkown: stories of women, colonized people, black authors, African authors, voices of community and community resistance to colonial power

MauMau: The Strength of the Pack is the Wolf

“This piece tells the story of the MauMau rebellion, rewriting the colonialist understanding of the Kenyans’ struggle for independence. Much of this work is dedicated to the factual and informative overview of the history of this revolution. The piece resists the colonial narrative, proving that actual history is more nuanced than that. And perhaps most importantly, this piece challenges the fact that the Mauau were a terrorist group, when really, they were freedom fighters. This is a narrative, yes, but a rearrangement of historical truth.”

For Kenyan Girls

This play tells the stories of six women who have suffered oppression in an extremely sexist society. As a theartical piece, this series of personal monologues tells interconnected stories of love, empowerment, and struggle and sheds light on the plight of women in the African society. The cast consists of seven NAMELESS women only identified by their role in society. They are: EDUCATED GIRL, PROSTITUTE, DROP-OUT, SINGLE MOTHER, VILLAGE GIRL and MARRIED GIRL. Subjects from forced marriage, abandonment, domestic violence and inferiority complexes are tackled. For Kenyan Girls is all about women empowerment: A fight against oppression.

Performances

Dance, Theatre, Choreopoems

During the summer of 2017, thanks to Lynk Funding (a Mount Holyoke College funding program that connects a student’s academic work with practical applications of the liberal arts and sciences), I wrote, directed and produced the play For Kenyan Girls in Nairobi, Kenya. The play told the stories of six women/girls who have suffered oppression in an extremely sexist society. Subjects from forced marriage, school drop-outs, domestic violence and inferiority complexes were tackled. The play was produced at Alliance Française in Nairobi on August 3rd and 5th, 2017. As I wrote the script and worked on the project throughout my time in Kenya, I realized that I am genuinely interested in the literary scene as a professional career.
